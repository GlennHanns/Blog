Your First Meeting with a Cinematographer

Glenn Hanns ACS  ·  Sydney

Most directors approaching their first or second feature carry a version of the same anxiety into the first meeting with a DOP: what do I actually say, what are they assessing, and how do I raise budget without killing the conversation before it starts?

The honest answer is that a good DOP is not waiting to catch you out. They’re trying to work out the same thing you are — whether this is a collaboration worth committing to.

Here’s what the first meeting actually looks like from my side of the table.

What I bring

I read the script before any meeting. Not to demonstrate preparation, but because walking in without having read it is a waste of both our time. By the time we sit down I’ll have a rough sense of the visual world the script is asking for — whether it wants to be handheld or locked, whether the light should feel controlled or available, what the emotional register of the film is.

I usually bring references. Not a polished lookbook — that comes later in prep if we decide to work together. But a few images, paintings, or films that the script made me think of. These aren’t proposals. They’re conversation starters. The director who comes back with “yes, exactly” or “no, the opposite” has already told me a great deal about how they think.

What I’m listening for

I’m not trying to confirm you’ve done your research or test your film literacy. I want to understand what kind of director you are.

Some directors have a precise visual language and want a DOP who can execute it technically. Others have a strong emotional sense of what the film should feel like but are open about not knowing how to get there visually. Most are somewhere between the two. None of these is a problem — but knowing which one you are helps me understand what you actually need.

I’m also listening for whether you want a collaborator or a technician. Both are legitimate working relationships. But they’re different conversations, and different productions.

The director who comes in curious rather than evaluating tends to produce a different kind of meeting — and usually, a different kind of film.

The questions you’re afraid to ask

Budget, rate, and availability are fair questions to lead with. Not raising them wastes everyone’s time — a conversation that goes three meetings deep before discovering a basic incompatibility serves nobody.

Similarly, asking about working speed is not an insult. A DOP who can’t talk plainly about how they move through a day isn’t someone you want running your camera department. I’d rather have that conversation early than discover on day three of a tight schedule that our expectations don’t align.

The same goes for crew. If you’re bringing your own people — a gaffer you’ve worked with, a camera operator you trust — say so. If you’re relying on the DOP to bring theirs, that’s worth establishing early too. The department a DOP builds around them is as much a part of what you’re hiring as the DOP themselves.

What the meeting isn’t

It isn’t a pitch. If I’m spending the meeting telling you how good I am, something has gone wrong. The reel exists to show the work — the conversation should be about the film.

It also isn’t an audition in the formal sense. The director who comes in with a checklist of technical questions — which camera, which lenses, what format — tends to get useful answers but miss the more important information: whether this person will actually work creatively alongside them under pressure.

Those technical decisions are made in prep, with the whole picture in front of you. What the first meeting can tell you is whether you’ll be able to make them together.

How to know if it’s the right fit

You should leave the meeting with a clearer sense of what the film looks like. Not a complete picture — that’s what prep is for — but some movement toward it. If the conversation stays abstract or theoretical the whole way through, that’s worth noting.

The other signal is simpler: did you feel like you could tell them when something wasn’t right? The DOP–director relationship runs on honest, sometimes uncomfortable creative conversations. If the dynamic in the first meeting doesn’t feel like it can hold that, it probably won’t hold it on set either.
